Regulatory reference · June 2026

The EU sovereign cloud stack

Three instruments that govern cloud for European public bodies, read as a building. NIS2 is the fire code every structure must meet. The SEAL framework is the surveyor's instrument that scores how sovereign a service is. CADA is the planning law that decides which buildings the state may buy. One sets a floor, one measures, one buys.

In force binding now Voluntary a tool, not yet a duty Proposal draft, subject to trilogue

The gate · on top

CADA

Cloud and AI Development Act

Proposal, 3 Jun 2026

A Regulation that codifies the sovereignty framework into four binding Union assurance levels, tied to public procurement, plus Union added value criteria and data-centre acceleration.

Purpose

Turn voluntary sovereignty scoring into a procurement gate, and expand EU compute capacity.

Who it binds

All cloud providers serving the EU public sector. The four tiers map onto SEAL.

Why it matters

Level 4 demands full EU ownership, EU-cleared personnel and no third-country interference. It is the point where sovereignty becomes a structural requirement rather than a contractual promise. US hyperscalers are locked out of Level 4 by CLOUD Act exposure.

Four Union assurance levels

L1Baseline. An EU entity, which may be the EU subsidiary of a non-EU firm, keeps infrastructure and data in the EU. Foreign-controlled providers must guarantee no duty to report unpatched vulnerabilities to a third country.
L2Substantial sovereignty. Third-country ownership still allowed, but all operations, infrastructure, personnel and support sit strictly within the EU. No foreign data access, no remote kill-switch, no coerced sanctions compliance.
L3High sovereignty. Third-country control prohibited by default. Owned and controlled from within the EU, with Union-citizen personnel. The Commission may, by exception, recognise an associated third country.
L4Maximum autonomy. Third-country control banned outright, with no associated-third-country exception. Highest cybersecurity certificate, plus full control over the software supply chain with no third-country influence.

Levels are set per workload by a member-state or Union-entity risk assessment, with L1 the default; the Commission expects only about 10 percent of public-sector cases to need L3 or L4. SEAL's levels map onto these four tiers. CADA is a proposal, with adoption targeted for late 2027, so this is the current draft.

The measure · in the middle

EU Cloud Sovereignty Framework / SEAL

Commission scoring tool (CSF)

Voluntary

A scoring methodology. SEAL ranks services from SEAL-0 to SEAL-4 across eight weighted SOV objectives.

Purpose

Give "how sovereign is this service" a rankable number for procurement.

Who it binds

Providers bidding for Commission and public-sector work. Scored the EUR 180m award in April 2026.

Why it matters

The first auditable definition of cloud sovereignty, precise enough to be ranked and used as a procurement filter. The S3NS award showed the scale can still be contested at the margins.

Five assurance levels

SEAL-0No sovereignty. Full dependence on third countries, no protection from extraterritorial intervention.
SEAL-1Jurisdiction. EU law applies to the contract, but technical control stays external.
SEAL-2Data sovereignty. Data and key management in EU hands. Tender minimum threshold.
SEAL-3Digital resilience. Immune to disruption from non-EU supply chains, EU staff can run it independently.
SEAL-4Full digital sovereignty. Complete EU supply chain, chip to software, no third-country dependency. Equivalent to CADA Level 4.

Eight SOV objectives, weighted to 100 percent

SOV-5 Supply chain20%
SOV-1 Strategic, EU ownership15%
SOV-4 Operational, EU staff15%
SOV-6 Technology, open and portable15%
SOV-2 Legal, insulation from foreign law10%
SOV-3 Data and AI10%
SOV-7 Security and compliance10%
SOV-8 Environmental5%

The highlighted objectives are those most shaped by ownership and architecture. The catch sits in the heaviest one: SOV-5 supply chain carries 20 percent and is the hardest for any European integrator to win, because it reaches down to chip origin.

The floor · on the ground

NIS2

Directive (EU) 2022/2555

In force

Cybersecurity baseline: risk management, incident reporting, supply-chain security, board accountability.

Purpose

Raise cyber hygiene across essential and important entities.

Who it binds

18 sectors: energy, transport, health, public admin, digital infrastructure.

Why it matters

A baseline obligation, not a sovereignty differentiator. Regulated-sector cloud has to meet it regardless of provider, which makes compliance table stakes rather than a selling point.

SecNumCloud

National

Note: the genetic ancestor of CADA's four-level logic. Several European providers already hold the qualification.

AI Act

Regulation (EU) 2024/1689

Phasing

Risk-based governance of AI systems, applying in stages to 2027.

Note: adjacent, but CADA borrows its "AI system" definition. Relevant once a platform hosts AI workloads.

How they fit: NIS2 and CER are the cyber and physical halves of one resilience regime over the same sectors. For finance, DORA applies in place of NIS2's ICT rules rather than on top of them. The practical catch: a directive only bites where a country has written it into national law, so coverage is patchy, while a regulation bites everywhere at once.
